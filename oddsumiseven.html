The sum of two odd integers is an even integer.

Let us prove that
The sum of any two odd integers is an even integer.

We give the first even integer the name \(m\).
We give the second even integer the name \(n\).
Thus, the sum is expressed by the name \(m+n\).
First, we must prove that \(m+n\) is an integer.
Second, we must prove that \(m+n\) is even.

Recall that the integers are closed under addition.
Since \(m\) and \(n\) are integers,
It then follows from this closure property
That \(m+n\) is an integer.
Thus we have shown the first of the two things that we were to prove.

Recall that \(n\) is odd.
By the definition of odd,
There is an integer \(k\)
Such that \(n=2k+1\).

Recall that \(m\) is odd.
By the definition of odd,
There is an integer \(l\)
Such that \(m=2l+1\).

Recall that \(m+n\) is the sum.
We may substitute \(2k+1\) for \(n\)
And \(2l+1\) for \(m\).
Thus, \(m+n=(2k+1)+(2l+1)\).

Recall the associative property of addition.
It says that for any real numbers \(x\), \(y\), and \(z\),
We may conclude that \((x+y)+z=x+(y+z)\).
Using \(2k\) for \(x\), \(1\) for \(y\) and \((2l+1)\) for \(z\),
It follows that \((2k+1)+(2l+1)=2k+(1+(2l+1))\).

Recall the commutative property of addition.
It says that for any real numbers \(x\) and \(y\),
We may conclude that \(x+y=y+x\).
It follows that \(2k+(1+(2l+1))=2k+((2l+1)+1)\).

We may use the associative property of addition again:
\(2k+((2l+1)+1)=2k+(2l+(1+1))\).
We note that \(1+1=2\):
\(2k+(2l+(1+1))=2k+(2l+2)\).
We may use the associative property of addition again:
\(2k+(2l+2)=(2k+2l)+2\).
We may use the distributive property of multiplication over addition
For the first time in this proof:
\((2k+2l)+2=2(k+l)+2\).
And we may use the distributive property of multiplication over addition once more:
\(2(k+l)+2=2((k+l)+1)\).

Now we know several things:
That \(m+n=(2k+1)+(2l+1)\)
And that \((2k+1)+(2l+1)=2k+(1+(2l+1))\)
And that \(2k+(1+(2l+1))=2k+((2l+1)+1)\)
And that \(2k+((2l+1)+1)=2k+(2l+(1+1))\)
And that \(2k+(2l+(1+1))=2k+(2l+2)\)
And that \(2k+(2l+2)=(2k+2l)+2\)
And that \((2k+2l)+2=2(k+l)+2\)
And that \(2(k+l)+2=2((k+l)+1)\)

We also know the transitive property of equality,
Which says that \(x=z\) when \(x=y\) and \(y=z\).
It follows that \(m+n=2((k+l)+1)\).

The end is near
Now that we know
\(m+n=2((k+l)+1)\).

Since the integers are closed under addition,
And since \(k\) and \(l\) are integers,
We may conclude that \(k+l\) is an integer.

Since the integers are closed under addition,
And since \(k+l\) and \(1\) are integers,
We may conclude that \((k+l)+1\) is an integer.
Let us say that \(p\) is this integer \((k+l)+1\).

Now that we know that \((k+l)+1\) is the integer \(p\),
And notice that \(m+n\) can be expressed as
Two times the integer \(p\).
We may apply the definition of even
To say and to know that \(m+n\) is even.
Thus we have shown the second of the two things that we were to prove.

Thus we have shown both of the two things that we were to prove.
We have shown that when we start with two odd integers \(m\) and \(n\),
Their sum, \(m+n\), is an even integer,
Quod erat demonstrandum.
